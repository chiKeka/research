# The Twin That Keeps Score

Late Monday night I asked the AI system I have been building for four months a question I had been avoiding. What is a thinking partner worth if it never finds out whether it was right? By two in the morning we had built the answer, and the answer now keeps score on both of us, in public.

Some context first. The people building this category have been saying loudly that the way we work with AI agents is changing shape. Peter Steinberger puts it as "you shouldn't be prompting coding agents anymore, you should be designing loops that prompt your agents." Boris Cherny, who runs Claude Code at Anthropic, says his job now is to write loops. The recipe going around has five ingredients, and they all ship inside the products now: scheduled automations, isolated worktrees so parallel agents do not collide, skills files that hold project knowledge, connectors into your real tools, and subagents so the one that writes is not the one that checks. Plus a sixth thing, a memory that lives outside any single conversation. Five primitives and a notebook, and the discourse treats that checklist as the whole recipe.

I run one of these systems. It is a cognitive twin: it scans news and research every morning, maintains a registry of named analytical frameworks I think with, drafts analyses, audits its own routing weekly, and writes everything to a git repository that functions as its memory. So I can report from the other side of the checklist, and the report is this: all five primitives, assembled correctly, still produced a system that quietly fell apart for a month.

In May every loop ran perfectly. The morning scans fired on schedule, the work was real, the commits were clean. And none of it landed. Each run wrote its results to an isolated throwaway branch that never merged, so the system's memory forked into 27 parallel versions of the truth while the main record froze for four weeks. My weekly audit reported green the entire time, because its verification layer had nothing to verify and skipping everything looks identical to passing everything. Twenty-seven branches, a month of accumulated intelligence, five weeks of checkmarks that meant nothing. I found it by accident.

That failure taught me the two primitives missing from the checklist. The first is convergence: isolation is a loan, and a loop iteration that does not end by reconciling its work back into shared memory did not happen, it just consumed tokens. The second is verifier liveness: a checker that cannot fail loudly when it has nothing to check will convert "nothing was verified" into "all good," and you will trust it precisely because it never bothers you. There is a third constraint underneath both, which is that the human's decision bandwidth is the real throughput limit of any loop system. My queue, not my agents, was the bottleneck; two finished pieces sat waiting on my go-ahead for five weeks. You do not fix that with more automation. You fix it by designing exactly what is allowed to wait for the human, and letting everything reversible flow.

We repaired all of that in one long day, and then the more uncomfortable problem surfaced. Every loop in the system closed on process or on style. The audits checked whether the machinery ran. The voice models checked whether the output sounded like me. Nothing, anywhere, checked whether any of it was right. My frameworks were scored by how often they got used, which means an elegant framework that is consistently wrong would survive governance forever. And the standard answer to AI slop, which is "read everything it produces," is a discipline answer, and discipline loses to throughput every time. The deeper issue is structural: human memory rewrites confidence after the outcome arrives. "I always suspected that" is what being wrong feels like from the inside. You cannot fix hindsight bias with self-awareness, because the bias edits the awareness.

So we built what I have started calling the epistemic ledger. Every falsifiable claim the system or I produce gets registered with a stated probability, the test that would resolve it, and a deadline. The probability locks at registration and is never edited. My published positions and the machine's own analyses are scored as separate populations, so the machine cannot launder its guesses into my track record. The morning scan checks each day's signals against the open claims, and any evidence that cuts against a position leads my morning brief, above the news, because confirmation finds you on its own and disconfirmation has to be forced to the top. A second tier of small, fast-resolving forecasts exists purely to train the system's calibration at a usable rate, and once enough of them resolve, the system computes its own corrections and applies them to its next estimates. A loop that ends in an artifact optimizes for plausible artifacts. A loop that ends in a scored claim optimizes for being right.

As of this week the whole board is public at github.com/chiKeka/ledger. Sixteen claims are open right now. The machine says 75 percent that the EU AI Act's simplification turn reduces enforceable obligations for frontier deployers by year end. I am on record at 55 percent that at least two more African states formally designate data centres as critical infrastructure in the same window. There is a 72 percent claim that aggregate productivity statistics will stay silent on AI through 2026 while firm-level concentration grows, which puts a deadline on the most expensive bet in the economy. The board updates itself without my involvement, the misses stay up, and the first resolution is already posted: within a day of going live, the system discovered that a Lagos data-centre milestone it was tracking had already been publicly commissioned, scored itself on it, and recorded a footnote admitting the question was settled before it asked. That footnote is the product working.

Here is the structural reason you have not seen this before. A vendor whose product can make its customer look wrong does not ship that feature, so no AI product keeps honest score on its user, and the feature stays unoccupied territory for any individual willing to absorb the embarrassment. That willingness is the entire moat. Two people can run identical loop systems and get opposite outcomes: one uses the loops to extend judgment they keep exercising, the other uses them to stop having an opinion at all. The loop cannot tell the difference. The exit condition can. End your loops in deliverables and you will drown in plausible output you no longer understand. End them in claims you must stand behind and the machine becomes the most honest colleague you have.

The question for the loop era is not how much your agents can do while you sleep. It is what, exactly, still requires you awake. My answer is on the board with probabilities attached, and from here the world does the grading.
